Executive Summary
The Community in Numbers

The 2019 State of the Map Africa conference, held in Grand-Bassam, Ivory Coast from 22–24 November, received 232 Call for Proposals submissions across the open window (July–November 2019) — drawing contributions from more than 40 countries and representing the full breadth of Africa’s open mapping ecosystem.

Nigeria led African contributions with 19 submissions, followed by Kenya (15), Tanzania and Uganda (10 each). France (7) was the highest non-African contributor, reflecting the active Francophone diaspora within the global OSM community. Of respondents who stated a region, 52% were based in Africa. However, a significant proportion of submissions did not state a region, suggesting a form-design opportunity for future editions.

Access and equity signals in the data are striking. Of respondents who answered the scholarship question, 66% applied for financial support — a finding that directly informed resource allocation decisions in subsequent editions. The bilingual conference design was validated: 37% of respondents proposed to present in French or bilingually, confirming that French-language CFP materials meaningfully expanded the submission pool.

The overall acceptance rate of 19.8% (46 sessions from 232 proposals) reflects a rigorous, quality-driven curation process. Small Group Discussions achieved the highest selection rate (29%), a deliberate programme design choice to prioritise participatory formats over lecture-style delivery.

Key Findings
What the Data Tells Us
Finding 01

Submission volume is concentrated in a narrow window

67% of all 232 proposals arrived in September and October alone, with October the clear peak (94 submissions — 41% of the total). This has direct implications for review committee capacity planning in future editions.

Finding 02

Financial access is the primary participation barrier

66% of respondents who answered the scholarship question applied for financial support — a funding gap argument that should be used actively in sponsor and donor conversations.

Finding 03

Bilingual design was justified and measurable

37% of respondents proposed to present in French or bilingually. Guinea’s strong showing (6 submissions) is direct evidence that French-language outreach produced real gains from communities that would otherwise not have submitted.

Finding 04

Geographic concentration creates future outreach priorities

Four countries accounted for 42% of African-stated submissions. Ivory Coast, as the 2019 host country, was notably under-represented in the submission data — a clear target for community outreach in future editions hosted in Francophone Africa.

This analysis draws on two primary datasets: the CFP submission form responses (232 records covering July–November 2019, 28 fields) and the programme selection workbook containing reviewer scores across five session format tracks. Eight metrics were selected based on their relevance to programme design, community equity, and operational planning.

Section 1
Submission Pipeline & Timeline

Submissions opened in July 2019 and closed ahead of the conference, which took place 22–24 November in Grand-Bassam. October was the peak submission month (94 proposals — 41% of the total). The surge pattern mirrors academic conference submission behaviour: contributors tend to wait for the final weeks of a CFP window before submitting, regardless of how early the window opens.

67% of all submissions arrived in September and October. Review committees should concentrate capacity in this window — and consider whether late deadline extensions inadvertently disadvantage less-resourced contributors who planned around the original date.
Figure 1 — CFP Submissions by Month (July–November 2019)

October was the dominant submission month, accounting for 41% of all proposals. July and August saw relatively low activity, confirming that extended CFP windows do not necessarily produce earlier submissions.

Section 2
Geographic Reach & Community Representation

The 2019 edition drew proposals from more than 40 countries, with Sub-Saharan Africa well-represented across the top contributors. Nigeria, Kenya, Tanzania, and Uganda together accounted for 54 of the Africa-stated submissions, reflecting the strength of the OSM community in East and West Africa.

France’s position among the top contributing countries underscores the importance of the global Francophone diaspora to SotM Africa. Guinea’s strong showing (6 submissions) is a direct signal that bilingual outreach is producing results in Francophone West Africa — a particularly relevant finding given that the 2019 conference was hosted in Ivory Coast.

Figure 2 — Submissions by Country: Top 14

African countries are shown in navy; non-African contributors in slate. Ivory Coast, as the host country, is notably absent from the top 14 — a finding that warrants attention in outreach planning for future Francophone-hosted editions.

Figure 3 — Submissions by World Region

52% of region-stated submissions came from Africa. The significant proportion of submissions without a stated region suggests the field was perceived as optional — a form-design issue to address in future editions to improve data completeness.

Section 3
Session Formats & Programme Design

Thirty-minute presentations dominated proposed formats (77 primary submissions — the largest single format category). Lightning talks were the second most popular (52). The relatively lower volumes for workshops and small group discussions reflect higher logistical requirements rather than low interest — both formats achieved above-average selection rates precisely because the programme committee prioritised them.

Figure 4 — Proposed Session Formats (Primary Classification)

Where submissions indicated multiple preferred formats, the primary preference was extracted. 30-minute presentations and lightning talks together accounted for over half of all proposals.

Section 4
Language Inclusion & Access Equity

Of respondents who stated a presentation language, 37% indicated French or bilingual capability. This validates the resource investment in bilingual conference design — a particularly important finding for a conference hosted in Ivory Coast, where French is the primary language. The scholarship demand data is the single most important equity finding in this dataset: 66% of respondents who answered the question applied for financial support.

Figure 5 — Presentation Language Preference

37% of respondents proposed bilingual or French-language presentations, confirming meaningful Francophone community engagement driven by the bilingual conference design.

Figure 6 — Access & Inclusion Indicators

Three equity indicators are shown: scholarship applications, collaborative sessions, and French/bilingual presenters. The “Not stated” proportion reflects incomplete form completion rather than absence of the relevant characteristics.

Section 5
Content Themes & Thematic Priorities

Cartography and visualisation, contribution and data collection, and community growth and diversity were the three most selected content categories — all core to the OSM identity and aligned with the conference theme. The presence of public health (22, 9% of submissions) and environment and climate (18, 8%) signals a broadening of the community’s application domains into development and humanitarian sectors.

Respondents could select multiple categories. Percentages represent the proportion of all 232 submissions selecting each category.

Section 6
Programme Curation & Selection Analysis

46 sessions were selected from 232 proposals — an 19.8% overall acceptance rate. Small group discussions were selected at a higher rate (29%) than any other format, reflecting the programme committee’s intentional effort to prioritise participatory formats consistent with the conference’s community values.

Figure 8 — Submitted vs. Selected Sessions by Format

Navy bars show total submissions; amber bars show selected sessions. 30-minute presentations produced the highest absolute number of selected sessions (22).

Figure 9 — Selection Rate by Session Format

Amber bars indicate formats with above-average selection rates. Small group discussions and workshops both achieved above-average rates, reflecting deliberate programme committee intent to diversify beyond lecture-style formats.

Recommendations
Implications for Future Editions

The following recommendations are drawn directly from the data findings and are intended to inform programme planning for subsequent State of the Map Africa conferences.

1

Build review capacity for the October surge

With 41% of proposals arriving in October, review committees should plan for concentrated workload in this window. A two-stage review process with a soft close in September would allow early feedback to submitters still finalising proposals.

2

Use scholarship demand data in funding conversations

A 66% scholarship application rate is a compelling case for sponsor and donor investment in travel grant pools. This figure should be included in all sponsorship proposals and grant applications for future editions.

3

Make the region field mandatory and restructure language options

A significant proportion of submissions did not state a region. Making the field required and restructuring the language field to clearly distinguish presentation language from comfort level would improve data completeness for future programme planning.

4

Target outreach to under-represented communities in host countries

Ivory Coast, as the 2019 host, was notably absent from the submission data. For future editions hosted in Francophone Africa, dedicated relationship-based outreach to local OSM chapter leads — beginning at least three months before the CFP opens — is likely to produce measurable gains in host-country participation.
